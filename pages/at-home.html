Home Learning Environments

At Home With Your Child

Research shows that the home environment plays a central role in children's language development. The good news? You do not need expensive resources. Your voice, your time and your stories are the most powerful tools (Yeo, Ong & Ng, 2014).

You Are Already Doing This

You are your child's first teacher

Research consistently shows that the quality and quantity of conversations at home, access to books, and opportunities for play and storytelling all shape how children learn to communicate. The best news? These do not require money or special training — just time and intention (Yeo, Ong & Ng, 2014).

6 Simple Tips

6 things you can do every day

Each of these is backed by research — and all of them can fit into your normal family routine.

📸

01
📖

Read together every day

Shared reading is one of the most powerful things you can do for your child's language development. It builds vocabulary, listening skills, comprehension and a love of stories. You do not have to read every word — talking about the pictures, making up voices and asking questions all count!

Try asking: "What do you think will happen next?" or "How do you think she is feeling?" or "Can you find the dog on this page?"

Even 10 minutes a day makes a significant difference. Visit your local library together — it is free and children can choose their own books.

📘 Research: Children from homes with rich literacy environments develop stronger reading skills and greater interest in learning (Yeo, Ong & Ng, 2014).

🎵

Sing songs and say rhymes

Singing nursery rhymes, silly songs and action songs helps children hear the patterns and sounds in language. This builds phonological awareness — a critical skill for learning to read. It does not matter if you are not a great singer. The repetition and rhythm are what count.

Try: Twinkle Twinkle, Heads Shoulders Knees and Toes, Old MacDonald, Baa Baa Black Sheep — or songs from your own cultural background. All build the same skills.

Clapping along to syllables in words is also a brilliant game — try clapping your child's name!

📘 Research: Children who regularly engage with songs and rhymes develop stronger sound awareness essential for reading (State Government of Victoria, n.d.).

03
💬

Have real conversations

Back-and-forth conversation — even short exchanges — is incredibly powerful for language development. During meals, bath time, the drive to school — these everyday moments matter enormously.

Turn off screens. Get down to your child's level. Ask open questions. Pause. Let them answer. Listen with your whole attention. That act of being heard teaches children that their voice matters.

Try: "What was the best part of your day?" or "If you could fly anywhere, where would you go?"

📘 Research: Conversational turn-taking is more strongly linked to children's brain development than simply hearing a large number of words (Romeo et al., 2018).

04
🎭

Encourage pretend play and storytelling

When children play pretend — playing house, being superheroes, acting out stories — they are practising using language to create worlds, take on roles, and express complex ideas. This builds vocabulary, grammar, narrative skills, imagination and empathy.

Join their play! Ask what is happening in the story, suggest a new character, or ask "What happens next?" Tell family stories together — children love hearing about when you were young.

📘 Research: Storytelling through play supports narrative development and forms the foundation for reading and writing (State Government of Victoria, n.d.).

05
📚

Create a reading corner at home

A cosy, accessible spot with books at child height sends a powerful message — reading is enjoyable and it is for you. It does not need to be a dedicated room. A basket of books by the sofa, a shelf in their bedroom, or even books in the car all count.

Keep a mix of picture books, rhyming books, information books and books in your home language. Rotate them to keep things fresh. Children who see adults reading are far more likely to develop their own love of reading.

📘 Research: Access to books at home is a significant predictor of early literacy success (Yeo, Ong & Ng, 2014).

06
🌍

Use your home language — it is a gift

If your family speaks a language other than English at home, keep using it. A strong foundation in the home language actually supports children in learning English and any additional languages. Speaking your home language is not confusing for children — it is a cognitive and cultural superpower.

Read books in your home language, tell stories from your culture, sing songs and lullabies you grew up with. These all build the language skills that transfer into English as well.

📘 Research: Children who maintain their home language alongside English develop stronger overall communication skills in both languages (Niklas, Tayler & Cohrssen, 2018; VCAA, 2020).

"Children's sense of belonging is strengthened when their language, interaction styles and ways of communicating are valued."
