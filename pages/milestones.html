Developmental Progressions

Growing Through Language

Every child develops language at their own pace — but there are typical patterns that help us understand what to expect and how to best support your child along the way (Johnston, 2010).

Age by Age

Language milestones from birth to school

Use this guide to understand typical development. Remember — all children develop at their own pace. If you have concerns, speak with your child's doctor or an early childhood professional.

Birth – 18 months
Early Communicators

What you might notice:

  • ✓Cooing and babbling from the earliest weeks
  • ✓Turning toward voices and familiar sounds
  • ✓Enjoying nursery rhymes, songs and being read to
  • ✓Attempting to sing or chant along with rhymes
  • ✓Showing interest in picture books
  • ✓Beginning to understand simple words (e.g. 'no', 'mama', 'bye-bye')

💙 What helps at this stage:

  • →Respond to every coo and babble — these are their first conversations
  • →Read simple picture books and talk about what you see
  • →Sing nursery rhymes and action songs every day
  • →Make eye contact and smile when talking to your baby
12 – 36 months
Early Language Users

What you might notice:

  • ✓First words appearing around 12 months
  • ✓Vocabulary growing rapidly — several new words a week
  • ✓Starting to combine two words (e.g. 'more milk', 'daddy go')
  • ✓Beginning to hear gaps between words in sentences
  • ✓Growing interest in syllables and rhymes
  • ✓Pointing at things and looking to you to name them

💚 What helps at this stage:

  • →Name everything around the house during daily routines
  • →Expand what they say — if they say 'dog', you say 'Yes! A big fluffy dog!'
  • →Read books with repetition and simple rhymes
  • →Sing simple songs with actions (Heads, Shoulders, Knees and Toes)
30 – 60 months
Language & Emergent Literacy Learners

What you might notice:

  • ✓Speaking in full sentences
  • ✓Telling simple stories with a beginning, middle and end
  • ✓Breaking words into syllables (el-e-phant)
  • ✓Beginning to recognise and produce rhymes
  • ✓Noticing words that start with the same sound (from ~36 months)
  • ✓Retelling events from their day

💚 What helps at this stage:

  • →Ask open questions: 'What do you think will happen next?'
  • →Encourage storytelling — tell family stories together
  • →Play rhyming and word games
  • →Clap syllables in words together
5+ years
School Ready Communicators

What you might notice:

  • ✓Using complex sentences and varied vocabulary
  • ✓Telling detailed stories with clear structure
  • ✓Engaging in real, extended conversations on a topic
  • ✓Understanding most language used around them
  • ✓Beginning to read and write

💜 What helps at this stage:

  • →Have real, extended conversations about their interests
  • →Read chapter books together and talk about the story
  • →Encourage retelling and summarising stories
  • →Visit the library regularly — let them choose their own books
Sound Awareness

Playing with sounds

Before children learn to read, they need to understand that language is made up of sounds. This is called phonological awareness — and it develops naturally through play!

What is phonological awareness?

It is the ability to hear, identify and play with the sounds in language. It includes recognising rhymes, clapping syllables, noticing words with the same starting sound, and eventually blending and segmenting sounds. It develops long before children start reading (NSW Government, 2025).

🎵

Rhyme awareness

Noticing that cat, hat and bat all sound alike. Develops from around 30 months through songs and rhyming books.

👏

Syllable awareness

Breaking words into beats — el-e-phant has three beats. A great game for 3–4 year olds using clapping!

🐍

Sound awareness

Noticing words that start with the same sound — "snake starts with sss!" Develops from around 36 months.

You do not need special materials. Singing songs, reading rhyming books and playing word games are the most effective tools available to any family.NSW Government (2025); State Government of Victoria (n.d.)
