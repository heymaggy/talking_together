Understanding Language Development

How Children Learn to Talk

Oral language — the ability to speak and listen — is the very foundation of literacy. Before children can read or write, they need strong spoken language skills. Here is what research tells us about how it develops.

The Basics

What is oral language?

Oral language is the system of spoken communication. It is the foundation of literacy — helping children collaborate, problem-solve, express their needs and succeed in all areas of life (Education Review Office, 2023).

👂

Listening

Also called receptive language — the ability to hear, process and understand what others say. It includes understanding words, phrases, and how people use language to make meaning together.

🗣️

Speaking

Also called expressive language — using words, sounds, gestures or signing to express thoughts and feelings. Children learn to speak by hearing language, imitating, experimenting and being gently supported.

💡

More Than Words

Oral language also includes vocabulary, grammar and conversational skills — taking turns, staying on topic, and reading social cues. These build the platform for reading and writing.

"Active listening and a strong foundation of oral language is key to ongoing and lasting literacy learning."

Early Years Learning Framework for Australia, V2.0 (AGDE, 2022, p. 57)

Receptive Language

Learning to listen

Listening is not just hearing sounds — it is actively making sense of what others are communicating. Children learn to listen by:

👁️

Being listened to

When adults give children their full attention, children learn that communication is valued and meaningful.

🔄

Real back-and-forth conversations

Even simple exchanges build strong listening skills. The to-and-fro of conversation teaches children how communication works.

🌍

Hearing language in meaningful contexts

Language learned during meals, play, routines and stories sticks much better than words heard in isolation.

(AERO, 2023)

Expressive Language

Learning to speak

Children learn to speak by:

  • ›
    Hearing rich language around them — the more varied and rich the language they hear, the more they absorb and use themselves.
  • ›
    Engaging in back-and-forth conversations — even responding to a baby's coo counts as a conversation. These serve-and-return interactions build language pathways in the brain.
  • ›
    Imitating and experimenting — making mistakes is part of the process! A child saying "I goed to the park" is showing they have learned grammar rules, even if they have not mastered the exception yet.
  • ›
    Being gently supported and extended — not corrected harshly, but modelled correctly. If they say "dog runned", you respond warmly: "Yes! The dog ran so fast!"
Research by Romeo et al. (2018) found that children who have more back-and-forth conversations show stronger language-related brain development — more so than simply hearing a large volume of words.Romeo et al. (2018)
Research Perspectives

What does research tell us?

Researchers have explored how children learn language from different angles. Here are three key ideas — and what each means for you as a family.

🔄
Behaviourist View — B.F. Skinner

Children learn by copying and being encouraged

When you repeat words back, celebrate your child's attempts, and gently model correct language, you are supporting learning through positive reinforcement. For example: your child says "dog runned" — you respond warmly "Yes! The dog ran so fast, didn't it!" This approach shows how everyday interactions build language (Napitupulu & Bako, 2024).

💡 What this means at home: Celebrate every attempt. Repeat and expand what they say rather than correcting.
🧬
Nativist View — Noam Chomsky

Children are born ready to learn language

Children come into the world already equipped to pick up language — they have an inbuilt capacity to learn the rules and patterns of any language they are exposed to. This means your job is to give them rich input: read to them, talk to them, use a wide variety of words. The more language they hear from you, the better (Barman, 2014).

💡 What this means at home: Read aloud every day. Use interesting, varied vocabulary. Talk about everything!
👥
Sociocultural View — Lev Vygotsky

Language grows through relationships

Children learn best through meaningful social interactions with people who care about them. When you talk together, ask open questions, tell stories, and join in your child's play, you are doing the most powerful thing possible for their language development. Children do not just learn words — they learn how to use language to think and to connect with others (McLeod, 2025).

💡 What this means at home: Talk with your child, not just at them. Ask open questions. Join their play. Tell family stories.
